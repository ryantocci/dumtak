About

A child's first word for music.

Dum Tak began with a one-year-old, his father's drum, and the two beats every culture in the world recognizes. This is how a family's morning ritual became a non-profit.

The first time my son said "Dum Tak," he wasn't yet a year and a half old. He couldn't say "drum." He couldn't say "music." But every morning his father played for him — a low dum on the bass of the hand, a sharp tak on the rim — and one day he repeated it back. Two syllables. The whole shape of rhythm in a single word.

I started paying attention after that. I noticed how his breathing slowed when we hummed. How he found the beat in a stirring spoon, in the windshield wipers, in his own footsteps. I noticed that he reached for music the way other children reach for blocks — as a thing to think with, not just listen to.

Then I started reading the research. What I found was that none of this was unusual. The first seven years of life are when the brain is most receptive to rhythm and music — and rhythm, it turns out, is the underlying architecture of language, memory, balance, and emotional regulation. The science was clear. What was missing was access.

Most of the families I knew didn't have a musician at home. Most preschools couldn't afford a music specialist. Most pediatric wards had no program at all. The most important developmental window in a child's life was being squandered, not because of a knowledge gap, but because of a delivery gap.

So we started showing up. First at one preschool, then a library, then a children's hospital. Working musicians, sitting on the floor, drumming with kids. No stage, no tickets, no auditions. Just the two beats my son taught me — and the children teaching themselves the rest.

— The founder

Real musicians.

Not recordings, not apps. Working artists, paid fairly, sitting on the floor with the kids they're playing for.

Science-led.
